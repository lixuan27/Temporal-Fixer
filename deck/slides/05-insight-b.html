Insight 4 · Co-adaptation

Once the editor is stabilized, the fixer's training distribution must be re-calibrated to the new draft statistics. A fixer trained for strong flicker over-corrects mild drafts: dynamic degree crushed to 0.167 (more static than the competitor we criticized), imaging quality down.

Re-matching the training degradation to the true draft statistics restored motion (0.375), restored quality to draft level, and produced the best edit preservation we have measured (0.959).

This is why five single-sided avenues failed. The ceiling was never in the fixer's loss — it was in the un-calibrated pair of stages.

Insight 5 · Flicker has a character, not just a magnitude

Measured spectrum of the frame-difference signal: identity reconstruction and a real mild edit share the same signature (high-temporal-frequency share ≈33%, luma/chroma balance ≈0.48) and differ only in amplitude (≈2.1 vs ≈7.4). An aggressive repaint is a different kind of flicker (48% high-frequency, chaotic).

Consequence for training data: match the kind with real reconstruction, match the amount with calibrated appearance-preserving jitter — never with a stronger prompt (that changes appearance and invalidates the target). See slide 10.

Insight 6 · Our own metrics were lying — so we rebuilt them

We briefly believed high dynamic degree = "motion-faithful". A source-anchored, style-invariant structure metric (RAFT flow from the source, DINOv2 patch tokens, floor = source-vs-itself) showed our high dynamic degree was flicker masquerading as motion. We retracted our own claim.

Inspiration: every deflickering number can also be produced by blurring or by reverting the edit. An instrument that cannot separate those three is not an instrument.
