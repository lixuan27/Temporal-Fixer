1 · Intro — New problem & new evaluation

Three of the four instruments did not exist. We built them.

Deflickering is trivially achievable by blurring, by freezing motion, or by reverting the edit. Any protocol that cannot separate those three from a real fix will reward the wrong model — ours included.

1 · Source-anchored structural consistency new metric

Optical flow is computed on the source (never on the output, which would be contaminated by the output's own flicker), features are DINOv2 patch tokens (style-invariant, so edit magnitude does not inflate it), occlusion-masked, and the floor is calibrated by source-vs-itself. It is the instrument that falsified our own motion-faithfulness claim.

2 · Flicker character spectrum new measurement

Beyond amplitude: high-temporal-frequency share, luma/chroma energy split, edge correlation. This turns "does my training flicker match my test flicker?" — the central train/test-gap question — from an opinion into a measurable, matchable quantity.

3 · Paired reading of smoothness and motion protocol correction

Standard video benchmarks reward stasis: a barely-moving video scores near-perfect on smoothness and subject consistency. We therefore always report motion preservation alongside smoothness, and treat any smoothness win at collapsed dynamics as disqualified.

4 · Anti-reversion gate mandatory

Every run reports edit preservation (CLIP to the draft) and on-promptness (CLIP to the instruction). A flicker reduction that comes with a preservation drop is a reverted edit, not a fix — and is rejected regardless of how good the temporal numbers look. Watch the video before reading the table.

Benchmarks. OpenVE-Bench (global style, 48 clips) · FiVE-Bench (colour + material, 40 clips) · VBench temporal dimensions · V2V-Bench (to add) · driving footage as the target domain. Baselines are re-run by us on identical videos under an identical protocol — never quoted from the competitor's own test set.
