Summary

A composition of two pretrained models, and one low-entropy thing to learn.

The claim

Streaming video editing does not need an edit-supervised video model. It needs a frozen per-frame image editor for content and a causal video model trained only to denoise temporal flicker — supervised entirely by clean video.

The mechanism that made it work

Co-adaptation. Diagnose the frozen editor's stochastic protocol first (it, not the edit, was producing the flicker), then re-calibrate the fixer's training distribution to the true draft statistics. Five single-sided interventions could not do what this one did.

The boundary we will not hide

Per-frame imaging quality is upper-bounded by the editor's re-render. We will improve it from the front end or state it as a limitation — we will not tune the fixer to fake it.

Immediate path

  • Now: source-free ablation, three arms — the gate is deflicker and non-reversion, judged on watched video.
  • Next: rebuild the reconstruction corpus on driving footage; retrain the winning arm.
  • Then: attach the rolling cache; measure the minute-scale drift curve and the per-chunk latency breakdown.
  • Finally: the same-videos head-to-head across both benchmarks plus driving, with motion always reported next to smoothness.

What would make this a paper, and what would make it a negative result

Paper: a temporal-consistency module that is editor-agnostic, needs no paired edit data, runs in one step, matches a dedicated streaming editor on temporal metrics while preserving more motion, and holds over minute-long streams.

Negative result: if the source-free arms cannot deflicker without reverting the edit, then appearance-preserving temporal denoising cannot be learned from reconstruction alone — and we will report that, with the mechanism, rather than dress it up.
